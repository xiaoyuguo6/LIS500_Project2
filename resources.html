What is Intersectionality?

Intersectionality is a framework that explores how different social identities like race, gender, and class overlap and create unique experiences of discrimination and privilege.

Learn more

What is Implicit Bias?

Implicit bias refers to unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions in an unconscious manner.

Learn more

What is IBT?

Implicit Bias Training (IBT) focuses on identifying and mitigating unconscious biases that may impact decision-making processes in professional and personal settings.

Learn more

Our Reflections on the Learning

Xiaoyu Guo: Learning about intersectionality opened my eyes to how multi-dimensional our identities are and how these dimensions interact to create unique experiences. I was struck by how society often simplifies these identities, focusing on just one aspect, like race or gender, when in reality, we’re all shaped by a combination of factors. This is especially true in tech, where people's experiences with AI and automation vary dramatically depending on their background. As a web developer, it’s crucial for me to understand this concept so I can design systems that are accessible and equitable for everyone. This also makes me reflect on my own position in the tech space, and how my identity may afford me privileges or obstacles that others don’t face. Going forward, I’ll aim to create digital experiences that take intersectionality into account, ensuring that no single group is prioritized over others. I also see the need for more education on this topic across the tech industry. Many of us tend to focus only on improving diversity metrics without addressing the complex, underlying systems of privilege that exist. This learning experience has shown me that being truly inclusive requires acknowledging and addressing these deep-rooted biases. I'll take these lessons into my future work, ensuring that inclusivity is a top priority in every project I take on.

Xiaoxue Lin: The discussion on implicit bias has really shifted my understanding of how unconscious biases can creep into our decision-making, especially in tech design. As someone who is interested in UX design, I often think about the user journey, but now I realize that my own biases could unconsciously influence design decisions, potentially making a product less inclusive. Implicit biases, whether related to gender, race, or ability, can manifest in the simplest choices—like which user scenarios we design for first or how we structure our language in user prompts. This reflection made me re-examine some of my own design projects. For example, I've been working on a health app, and I now wonder if it inadvertently favors certain demographics over others. I learned that it's not just about being aware of bias but actively working to counter it. That’s why IBT (Implicit Bias Training) has become so crucial. It gives us the tools to recognize and mitigate these biases. But beyond training, the next step for me is to continuously self-reflect and invite diverse perspectives into my design process to ensure that what I create is as inclusive as possible. I feel empowered by this learning experience, as it reminded me that small, conscious changes can have significant impacts on the inclusivity of a product. My goal now is to ensure that every project I work on actively addresses and mitigates implicit bias from start to finish.

Yixin Ye: Implicit Bias Training (IBT) and intersectionality both helped me understand the broader implications of AI and machine learning in society. The biases that we unconsciously hold are often replicated in the algorithms and AI systems we create, and this perpetuates inequality in ways that are often invisible. In the past, I would hear terms like 'bias in AI' and think it was just about improving the algorithm’s accuracy. But this course showed me that the issue is far more systemic. It’s about who gets to define what is “normal” in the first place. In AI training data, certain groups—whether by race, gender, or class—are often underrepresented, and this leads to skewed results when those algorithms are applied. This made me think critically about how we as developers and scientists have a responsibility to mitigate these biases at the root, ensuring that AI and machine learning serve everyone fairly. The more I learn about AI ethics, the more I realize how much we still need to work on in terms of fairness and equity. I’m committed to making sure I do my part in building a fairer tech landscape moving forward.
